## Overview

Two priorities in sequence. Stasis retention first — it's the highest-growth brand with the clearest lifecycle gap and needs its own engine, not borrowed Thesis patterns. Thesis retention second — a triage pass to implement quick wins now and scope the rest into a clear roadmap.

By Day 90, both brands have functioning lifecycle engines, the team structure reflects what an AI-first function actually needs, and retention is reporting a number — not a narrative.

### Stasis: Early Lifecycle Work

The first month of a Stasis subscription is where the retention curve is won or lost. A subscriber who understands what they're taking, why, and what to expect at week two versus week eight is meaningfully more likely to refill — and to commit to a longer plan before they reach that decision.

After the product walkthrough with Molly (Week 2), brief and commission Antidote on Stasis's post-purchase education series — mechanism, clinical expectations, habit formation, and a well-timed prompt for monthly subscribers to move to a longer commitment before the first renewal. Content briefed by Day 20. In build by Day 30.

## 60 Days

**Primary Focus:** Stasis lifecycle foundation is live; the at-risk and SMS layers get built. Thesis gets a triage pass with quick wins active. Team structure is locked.

---

### Stasis Retention

The post-purchase education series is live. Day 60 adds coverage for subscribers who didn't make it through month one and establishes SMS as a lifecycle channel.

- **At-risk re-engagement**: No flow currently exists for Stasis subscribers showing disengagement signals before their second refill. Build it with a proper audience definition and a holdout.
- **Winback**: Rebuild once the ReCharge → Klaviyo cancellation reason feed is confirmed working. Reason-specific messaging, holdout in place before launch.
- **Lifecycle SMS**: SMS has been promotional blast only. A post-purchase complement is the minimum — built with suppression rules before anything goes live.

---

### Thesis Retention

- **Hormesis Transition cleanup**: Six simultaneous flows sending to product migration cohorts. This should be one flow with routing logic. It's accumulated debt and gets resolved.
- **M1 Onboarding**: Strong open rate (57%), low click rate (1.3%). The subject lines work; the content and calls-to-action don't drive behavior. Rewrite the CTAs and add hooks toward meaningful second-order actions.
- **Cross-sell Stasis to Thesis subscribers**: High-LTV, high-fit audience. Dan confirmed appetite. No new flow architecture required — segment and message.
- **Subscription tenure segmentation**: New vs. 3mo+ vs. 6mo+ subscribers likely need different messages. Build the segment foundation.

---

### Operating Foundation

- **Claude Design**: Not yet configured for lifecycle. Set it up. This is the fastest path to scaling email creative output without adding headcount.
- **Campaign performance baseline**: Pull trailing 90-day click rates for both brands, set targets, and start measuring everything against them.

---

### Team and Agency Decision

By Day 60, the resource recommendation is implemented — not still in discussion. Antidote continues with clear KPIs and a weekly review cadence, or the transition is in motion. Michele decision is made. The operating model is clear.

## 90 Days

**Primary Focus:** Retention is reporting a measurable number. The function runs without Bryce owning every output.

---

### Measurable Retention Impact

- Pull the retention curve for Stasis subscribers acquired after the lifecycle series launched. Compare 30-day retention to the pre-intervention baseline — first real read on whether the engine is working.
- For Thesis: measure the impact of the quick wins from Days 30–60. Hormesis cleanup, M1 rewrite, cross-sell — what moved?
- Present a scorecard to Dan and Meredith: current vs. baseline on churn rate, 30-day retention, winback conversion, and MRR.

---

### Customer Portal and Save Strategy

- Use Theo to pull a competitive analysis of 15–20 DTC brand portals, cancellation flows, and save sequences. The cancellation video project that performed well hasn't been revisited in over a year — this is what refreshes it.
- The ReCharge cancellation test running now addresses one reason ("too expensive"). Build reason-specific save responses for the top three cancellation reasons — each deserves a different intervention.
- Deliver a portal improvement recommendation to the product team, ranked by effort and impact. Engineering dependency means portal changes may ship after Day 90 — the goal is to have the recommendation ready and prioritized.

---

### Loyalty and Referral

Loyalty doesn't exist. Referral isn't working. Both are cross-functional investments — the 90-day goal is a clear brief that makes the decision easy, not a launch.

- **Loyalty**: What does a program need to deliver in incremental retention to justify the cost? What are the mechanics options? Recommendation with a measurement plan.
- **Referral**: Diagnose what's broken before designing a replacement. Fix the constraint first.

---

### Reporting and Infrastructure

- Core retention metrics pulling automatically from Snowflake — churn, 30/90-day retention, MRR, winback conversion. The goal is Theo surfacing retention changes without manual work.
- Weekly retention digest operational and running.
- Structured weekly readout from Amrita on cancellation reasons and friction themes. Lifecycle roadmap is informed continuously.

## What This Plan Is Not

- **App development.** Dan is managing the app project. Lifecycle will contribute feedback when asked.
- **Content or organic social.** The content machine and podcast are Dan-directed. Lifecycle consumes that output — it doesn't produce it.
- **Retail or Amazon.** Mass retail is ~April 2027. Channel-aware cohort thinking is a Q3/Q4 conversation.
- **Fixed.** If the first 30 days reveal something that changes the sequence, the plan gets updated.
