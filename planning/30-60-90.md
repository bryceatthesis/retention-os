## Overview

The core retention problem at Thesis is structural: cancellations have held at a persistent floor of roughly 4,900–5,800/month for nearly a year regardless of how much the company acquires. Revenue has stayed stable because the recurring base acts as a floor — but that will not hold indefinitely. The opportunity is large and the account has been lightly managed.

This plan is organized around two sequential priorities:

**Priority 1 — Stasis Retention (Day 1):** Dan identified Stasis as the biggest retention opportunity and the highest-growth brand. It needs its own lifecycle engine. That work starts immediately and should be generating measurable results by Day 60.

**Priority 2 — Thesis Retention (Day 30–60):** Thesis retention has dropped significantly and needs a triage pass — quick wins where cost/benefit is clear now, a fuller rebuild where it's not. The goal by Day 90 is to have a clear, resourced roadmap even if the full rebuild extends beyond this period.

By Day 90, both brands have functioning lifecycle engines, the team and agency structure reflects what's actually needed for an AI-first function, and retention is on a trajectory with a clear measurement story.

### Stasis Retention: First Flows Live

Stasis is the priority. It is growing fastest, has the strongest moat, and per Dan, its retention engine needs to be built from scratch — not borrowed from Thesis patterns.

**Flows to build or rebuild by Day 60:**

- **Stasis winback**: Rebuild the existing winback flow with proper eligibility rules, holdout group, offer tiers within policy, and a clear measurement window. The current flow (`WEFcjQ`) converted at 0.21% — there is nowhere to go but up.

**Each new or rebuilt flow goes through `playbooks/lifecycle-audit.md` before launch.**

**Stasis-specific segments to build** (follow `playbooks/new-segment-launch.md`):
- Stasis active subscribers (not just L30 purchasers)
- Stasis at-risk (engagement-signal based)

### Thesis Retention: Triage and Quick Wins

The goal is not a full Thesis rebuild by Day 60. It is: identify the highest-leverage fixes, implement the ones where ROI is clear now, and scope the rest into a funded roadmap.

**Triage framework:** For each gap (missing flow, broken flow, segment gap), assess: effort, audience size, estimated revenue impact, and dependency on other work. Fix anything that can be done in a week and moves the needle. Everything else gets sequenced.

**Likely quick wins to evaluate:**
- M1 Onboarding: 57% open / 1.3% click is a direct response problem. Rewrite CTAs and calls-to-action; add a behavioral hook to drive portal engagement or second-order behavior. This is a Claude + Kristin project, not an engineering project.
- OTP → Subscription conversion: One-time purchasers are tracked but not systematically converted. Size the cohort. If large enough, build a targeted flow with a clear offer.
- Cross-sell Stasis to Thesis subscribers: Dan confirmed appetite for this. Thesis subscribers who don't have Stasis are a high-LTV, high-fit audience. Design the segment and message — this does not require a new flow architecture.

**Thesis-specific segments to build:**
- Active subscribers by formulation (Clarity, Motivation, Stress Reset, Neuroprotection)
- Subscription tenure bands (new / 3mo+ / 6mo+) — required for lifecycle strategy by cohort

### Measurable Retention Impact

By Day 90, there is a number. Not a narrative — a number.

- Pull the cohort retention curve for Stasis subscribers acquired since the new lifecycle flows went live (Day 60 cohort). Compare 30d retention to the pre-intervention baseline. This is the first real signal on whether the lifecycle engine is working.
- For Thesis: document the measurable impact of each quick win implemented in Days 30–60. If winback conversion moved from 0.21% to anything measurably higher, quantify it. If OTP→Sub flow is live, pull conversion rate.
- Present a retention scorecard to Dan and Meredith: current vs. baseline on voluntary churn rate, 30d retention, winback conversion, and MRR churn.

---

### Lifecycle SMS

- **Design the post-purchase SMS lifecycle**: At minimum, a Stasis onboarding SMS complement to the email series, and an at-risk re-engagement SMS. SMS has been 100% promotional blast to date with list-damage risk (5.15% unsub on the January blast). This is not a campaign SMS expansion — it is a lifecycle SMS build, with suppression and cadence discipline.
- **Rebuild list health rules**: Suppression tiers are partially in place (Antidote built them for email). Extend the logic to SMS before sending any lifecycle SMS.
- **Holdout every SMS flow**: SMS is a noisy channel. Nothing goes live without a holdout to measure incremental impact.

---

### Customer Portal and Save Strategy

- **Competitive audit**: Use Theo to pull screenshots of 15–20 DTC brand portals, cancellation flows, and save offer sequences into a Figma file. The cancellation video project Dan did a year ago has not been revisited. This is the foundation for a portal improvement project.
- **Cancellation save flow**: Map the current cancel → save sequence. What is the current save rate? What offers are being made? What is the policy? Rebuild the save flow with defined offer tiers, a holdout, and a measurement window.
- **At minimum by Day 90**: A recommendation delivered to May (Stasis PM) and Josh (Thesis PM) on the highest-leverage portal improvements, ranked by effort and estimated retention impact. Engineering dependency means portal changes may ship after Day 90 — the goal is to have the recommendation ready and prioritized.

---

### Loyalty and Referral Program Design

Loyalty doesn't exist. Referral isn't working. These were both in Dan's original list of gaps. They are high-effort, cross-functional initiatives — the 90-day goal is not to launch them, it is to deliver a defensible business case that makes the investment decision clear.

- **Loyalty**: Define the business case. What would a loyalty program need to deliver in incremental retention (and by how much) to justify its cost? Map the mechanics options (points, tiers, subscription perks, milestone rewards). Recommend a direction with a measurement plan.
- **Referral**: Diagnose why the current program isn't working before designing a new one. Audience? Offer? Technical friction? Fix the known constraint first; don't rebuild the program and repeat the same mistake.
- Deliverable: a one-page brief for each, with hypothesis, proposed design, cost/benefit estimate, and measurement plan. Present to Dan.

The goal Dan articulated — Theo auto-pulling from Snowflake and updating the retention view without manual work — should be at least partially functional by Day 90.

- **Klaviyo operating system**: Skills, segment definitions, and flow documentation should be current enough that any flow change can be made with confidence about dependencies.

## What This Plan Is Not

- **This is not an app development plan.** Dan is managing the app project (Stasis Kids initially), with an intern working on the first round. Bryce will stay informed and contribute feedback when asked, but lifecycle is not the owner of this track.
- **This is not a content or organic social plan.** The content machine and podcast initiative are in motion under Dan's direction. Lifecycle will consume that content (newsletter, educational flows, product stories) but is not responsible for producing it.
- **This is not a retail or Amazon plan.** Mass retail is likely April 2027. Amazon is scaling but owned by the channel manager coming on. Lifecycle will need channel-aware cohorts eventually — that is a Q3/Q4 conversation.
- **This is not a fixed resource commitment.** If a quick win in the first 30 days reveals a larger structural problem that changes the 60-day plan, the plan gets updated. Retention reporting will show what's working.
